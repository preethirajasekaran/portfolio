About me

In brief

I am a computer scientist turned behavioural ecologist, interested in understanding how behavioural variation arises and can influence decision making in animal conservation regimes. In the fall of 2019 I started my PhD in Kavita Isvaran’s lab at the Indian Institute of Science’s Centre for Ecological Sciences.

Our work broadly focuses on behavioural variation among individuals and plasticity in the lizard - South Indian Rock Agama (Psammophillus dorsalis). I am currently working on exploring how selection influences the plasticity of behaviour expression in these colourful lizards.

My work in supported by the Prime Minister’s Research Fellowship from MHRD, India and Indian Institute of Science, Bengaluru.

Current Project

Male agama (Credits: Shreekant Deodhar)

This colourful lizard above is the peninsular rock agama. It exhibits characteristic behaviours similar to that of many agamid lizards. Points of interest to the curious eye, would be its ability to change colours dynamically and the size difference between the sexes. On noticing these lizards for longer, one would see stereotypic behaviours directed towards members of its species - with nuanced differences with respect to the sex and age of the audience.

These stereotypic behaviours, on study have been proven to have levels of consistency at the individual level. Of the five personality (a measure of this consistency) axes,

  1. Activity
  2. Aggression
  3. Exploration-Avoidance
  4. Boldness-Shyness
  5. Sociability

work has been done on 2 and 4. The contrast between these 2 traits/behaviours is striking and forms the crux of our question. Boldness (measured using the proxy of Flight Initiation Distance/FID), is predominantly under natural selection i.e. it affects the survival of the organism. Aggression (measured using the proxy of body movements and encounters), are predominantly under sexual selection, as they are expressed in the context of mate choice and male-male competition - both of which contribute indirectly to the mates you acquire and the viability of your offspring.

Our question hence is derivative of this difference - is there a difference in the consistency of traits under sexual selection and natural selection? Would we expect traits under sexual selection to be more consistent or traits under natural selection?

Note: Every trait is under multiple levels and forms of selection, here we speak about relative influence.
